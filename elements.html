Design Elements

Design elements are the building blocks of visual composition. They shape how a user perceives information.

Form

Form describes the "3D-ness" of an object. Form can be achieved through the use of shadows, lighting, and overlapping elements in your design. Form creates visual interest and can make a design pop.

Shape

Shapes can be described as an enclosed line. Shapes come in two forms: geometric and organic. Geometric shapes are the ones we often see in math class; squares, triangles, circles, and other common 2D figures. Organic shapes, however, are more unique. These shapes are characterized by their free-form look and are harder to define than geometric shapes. Geometric shapes provide clean, sharp edges, while organic shapes feel more natural and loose.

Line

Lines are a figure created by at least 2 points. Lines are foundational to other elements such as shape and form; a design can be affected by the line weight, texture, direction, and more.

Color

Colors breathe life into a design when done correctly. Having a strong color palette can make a design more effective, but having a weak color combination can also drag down a design. It's important to understand color theory and color relationships before committing to a color scheme.

Texture

Texture is the surface of something: what material it might be, or if it's jagged or soft. When done right, texture can spark visual interest within a design, and also goes hand-in-hand with form to create 3D appearances.

Type

Type refers to the text on your design. It also guides the reader through visual hierarchy, such as titles, headings, copy (a fancy word for paragraphs or normal text).

Space

Space is defined by negative/white space; these are the areas in your design that remain empty and allow the other aspects to breathe. Compact designs can feel claustrophobic and overwhelming, so having negative space makes it visually appealing and better for readability.
